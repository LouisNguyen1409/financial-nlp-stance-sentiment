\begin{abstract}
We study two financial-NLP classification tasks on distinct text domains:
monetary-policy \emph{stance} (hawkish / dovish / neutral) on FOMC
communications \cite{shah2023trillion} and three-class \emph{sentiment} on the
Financial PhraseBank all-agree split \cite{malo2014good}. Starting from a
TF-IDF + logistic regression baseline, we build a ladder of twelve systems
that spans linear classifiers, the Loughran--McDonald lexicon, zero-/few-shot
probes over FinBERT / BERT / RoBERTa, full fine-tuning, and a multi-task
FinBERT with a shared encoder and two task heads. Our multi-task FinBERT
reaches \textbf{97.79\%} accuracy (\textbf{0.9666} macro-F1) on sentiment and
\textbf{64.92\%} accuracy (\textbf{0.6384} macro-F1) on stance. A carefully
fine-tuned \emph{general-purpose} BERT-base with layer-wise learning-rate
decay, gradual unfreezing and label smoothing \emph{ties} the multi-task
model (97.57\% / 0.9670 sentiment; 65.12\% / 0.6383 stance). Sentiment is
near-saturated; stance--especially the dovish/hawkish boundary--remains the
harder problem and is where domain pre-training and multi-task learning
still pay off in low-data regimes.
\end{abstract}

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Financial text analysis has become a de-facto downstream benchmark for
domain-adapted language models \cite{araci2019finbert, yang2020finbert}. Two
tasks dominate the practitioner literature. \emph{Sentiment} classification
on corporate news, exemplified by the Financial PhraseBank (FPB)
\cite{malo2014good}, asks whether a sentence conveys a positive, negative
or neutral outlook for an investor. \emph{Stance} classification on central
bank communications, framed by \citet{shah2023trillion} as the
Hawkish--Dovish problem, asks whether a sentence signals a tightening
(\emph{hawkish}), easing (\emph{dovish}) or non-committal (\emph{neutral})
monetary-policy posture. The two problems share surface vocabulary yet
differ sharply: sentiment is strongly lexical (``record profit'', ``loss''),
while stance depends on modal hedging and context (``some participants
noted'', ``price pressures have eased'').

This paper reports a controlled comparison of twelve systems on both tasks
using the public \texttt{gtfintechlab} Hugging Face splits.  Our
contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.15em,topsep=0.2em]
    \item A \emph{wide baseline ladder} TF-IDF with logistic regression,
          LinearSVC, trigrams, and hand-crafted Loughran--McDonald lexicon
          features establishing a strong non-neural floor.
    \item A zero-/few-shot study over three encoders (FinBERT, BERT-base,
          RoBERTa-base) that isolates the contribution of domain
          pre-training with almost no labelled data.
    \item A \emph{multi-task FinBERT} with a shared encoder and two linear
          heads, trained by alternating one stance batch and one sentiment
          batch per iteration.
    \item A second extension that fine-tunes a \emph{general-purpose}
          BERT-base with layer-wise learning-rate decay (LLRD), gradual
          unfreezing and label smoothing, which we find \emph{ties} the
          multi-task domain model on both tasks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\textbf{Domain lexicons.} \citet{loughran2011liability} showed that
general-purpose sentiment word lists systematically mis-label negative
corporate 10-K language; their Loughran--McDonald (LM) dictionary remains
the standard lexicon in financial NLP and we use it both as a stand-alone
classifier and as additional features to TF-IDF.

\textbf{Pre-trained transformers for finance.} BERT \cite{devlin2019bert}
and its descendants have been re-pre-trained on financial corpora: FinBERT
\cite{araci2019finbert} and the Reuters-TRC2 FinBERT variant
\cite{yang2020finbert} use domain masked-LM objectives and fine-tuning on
FPB. \citet{shah2023trillion} introduced the FOMC Hawkish--Dovish dataset
and showed that domain pre-training materially helps central-bank stance
prediction.

\textbf{Multi-task learning.} \citet{liu2019mt-dnn} demonstrated that
jointly training on multiple GLUE tasks improves each individual task via a
shared encoder. We port the same idea to finance with two heads on a
shared FinBERT encoder.

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{FOMC Hawkish--Dovish (stance)}
\label{sec:data-fomc}
We use \texttt{gtfintechlab/fomc\_communication} \cite{shah2023trillion},
2{,}480 sentences from Federal Open Market Committee statements, press
conferences and minutes, each annotated \emph{dovish}, \emph{hawkish} or
\emph{neutral}. The test split (496 sentences) is moderately
neutral-heavy: 130 dovish / 121 hawkish / 245 neutral
(26.21 / 24.40 / 49.40~\%).

\subsection{Financial PhraseBank all-agree (sentiment)}
\label{sec:data-fpb}
We use \texttt{gtfintechlab/financial\_phrasebank\_sentences\_allagree},
configuration \texttt{"5768"} \cite{malo2014good}: 2{,}264 financial news
sentences on which \emph{all five} annotators agreed. The test split (453
sentences) is neutral-dominant: 61 negative / 278 neutral / 114 positive
(13.47 / 61.37 / 25.17~\%).

\subsection{Loughran--McDonald lexicon}
\label{sec:data-lm}
We curate five word lists from \cite{loughran2011liability}:
LM-\textsc{positive} ($\approx$110), LM-\textsc{negative} ($\approx$230),
LM-\textsc{uncertainty} ($\approx$90) and two monetary-policy lists,
\textsc{hawkish} ($\approx$40) and \textsc{dovish} ($\approx$50). For each
sentence we extract 8 normalised features (counts divided by total words
plus the net polarity), \texttt{StandardScaler}-scale them and
\texttt{hstack} them to the TF-IDF matrix.

\subsection{Data splits}
\label{sec:data-splits}
For both corpora we take a 70/10/20 stratified split with
\texttt{random\_state=42}: FOMC 1736 / 248 / 496; FPB 1584 / 227 / 453. All
models see exactly the same test sentences.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Traditional baselines}
\label{sec:method-tfidf}

\subsubsection{TF-IDF + Logistic Regression (required baseline)}
We vectorise with
\texttt{TfidfVectorizer(max\_features=50000, ngram\_range=(1,2), sublinear\_tf=True)}
and classify with
\texttt{LogisticRegression(max\_iter=1000, class\_weight="balanced")}.

\subsubsection{TF-IDF + LinearSVC}
Same features, \texttt{LinearSVC(max\_iter=2000, class\_weight="balanced",
C=1.0)}. A linear margin classifier serves as a strong sparse-features
baseline without introducing kernels.

\subsubsection{TF-IDF trigrams + LR}
\texttt{ngram\_range=(1,3)}, \texttt{max\_features=80000},
\texttt{min\_df=2}; same LR. Adds longer phrases that matter for
monetary-policy wording (``target range'' + ``federal funds rate'').

\subsubsection{TF-IDF + LM lexicon}
We concatenate TF-IDF with the 8 LM lexicon features of
\S\ref{sec:data-lm} and train LR; this tests whether hand-crafted
financial signals complement surface $n$-grams.

\subsection{Pre-trained zero- and few-shot probes}
\label{sec:method-probe}
For \emph{zero-shot}, we use the native FinBERT 3-class sentiment head; for
stance we map the native labels
\{positive, negative, neutral\} $\rightarrow$ \{hawkish, dovish, neutral\}.
For \emph{few-shot} with $k=16$ examples per class (48 total) we freeze
each encoder, extract \texttt{[CLS]} embeddings, and train a linear probe
\texttt{nn.Linear(768,3)} with Adam, lr~$=10^{-3}$, 200 epochs. We probe
three encoders: FinBERT, BERT-base-uncased, RoBERTa-base.

\subsection{Fine-tuning}
\label{sec:method-ft}

\subsubsection{FinBERT full fine-tune}
\label{sec:method-finbert}
We fine-tune \texttt{ProsusAI/finbert} with AdamW, lr~$=2\times10^{-5}$,
weight decay 0.01, linear warmup over the first 10\% of steps followed by
linear decay, 5 epochs, gradient clip 1.0. Stance uses weighted cross
entropy with class weights $(1.272, 1.365, 0.675)$ for
(dovish, hawkish, neutral). We keep the checkpoint with the best
\emph{validation} macro-F1.

\subsubsection{BERT-base with LLRD and gradual unfreezing}
\label{sec:method-bert}
Our second extension fine-tunes \texttt{bert-base-uncased} (no financial
pre-training). We partition the model into 14 parameter groups from the
head (pooler+classifier) down through each of the 12 encoder layers to the
embedding table. Each group $d$ (head $= 0$) uses
\begin{equation}
\eta_d = 2\times10^{-5} \cdot 0.9^{\,d},
\end{equation}
so deeper transformer blocks receive smaller updates (LLRD). We further
apply \emph{gradual unfreezing}: at epoch $t$ only the top $t$ groups have
\texttt{requires\_grad=True}; the optimiser is rebuilt each epoch.
We train for 10 epochs with label smoothing $\varepsilon = 0.1$ (plus
class weights for stance), weight decay 0.01 everywhere except the head
(0.0), and \emph{no} separate warmup scheduler--gradual unfreezing itself
acts as implicit warmup.

\subsection{Multi-task FinBERT (main extension)}
\label{sec:method-mt}
We implement \texttt{MultiTaskFinBERT(nn.Module)} with a
shared FinBERT encoder
(\texttt{self.encoder = AutoModel.from\_pretrained("ProsusAI/finbert")}),
shared \texttt{nn.Dropout(0.1)} and two task-specific linear heads
\texttt{stance\_head = nn.Linear(768, 3)} and
\texttt{sentiment\_head = nn.Linear(768, 3)}. The \texttt{forward(\dots,
task)} method selects the head by task name. Training alternates one
stance batch then one sentiment batch per iteration for 8 epochs with
AdamW (lr~$=2\times10^{-5}$); stance uses class-weighted cross entropy,
sentiment uses standard CE. The checkpoint is selected by average
validation macro-F1.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}
We evaluate on the held-out test splits with accuracy and macro-F1
(sklearn \texttt{classification\_report}). Reproducibility is enforced by
\texttt{SEED=42} in \texttt{config.py} and \texttt{torch.manual\_seed(42)}
in every training script. All transformer training runs on an Apple M3 Max
GPU via the \texttt{torch.device("mps")} backend; the same scripts support
CUDA (H200). We use \texttt{transformers}, \texttt{datasets},
\texttt{scikit-learn} and \texttt{torch}.

\section{Analysis of Results}
\label{sec:analysis}

\paragraph{(1) Sentiment is near-solved; stance is not.}
Every transformer we train or probe clears 97\% sentiment accuracy,
including a zero-shot FinBERT that has \emph{never} seen our training
sentences (0.9735 / 0.9650). The TF-IDF+SVM baseline is already at 0.8940
/ 0.8534. On stance the story is different: the best model we could build
tops out at 0.6512 accuracy and 0.6384 macro-F1. The 33-percentage-point
gap between the two tasks (Figure~\ref{fig:progression}) reflects a
genuine difference in difficulty, not a capacity shortfall.

\paragraph{(2) Domain pre-training pays off most in low-data regimes.}
With only 48 labelled examples, FinBERT reaches 0.9670 sentiment macro-F1
while BERT-base (0.6500) and RoBERTa-base (0.6722) trail by
\emph{$\approx$30 points}. Once we allow full fine-tuning on the 1{,}584
FPB training sentences, the gap almost disappears: fine-tuned FinBERT is
0.9459 and BERT-base LLRD is 0.9670. Domain pre-training is a large-data
substitute, not a large-data amplifier.

\paragraph{(3) Multi-task learning helps stance more than sentiment.}
Multi-task FinBERT beats single-task fine-tuned FinBERT by
\textbf{+1.90~pp} stance macro-F1 (0.6194 $\rightarrow$ 0.6384) and
\textbf{+2.07~pp} sentiment macro-F1 (0.9459 $\rightarrow$ 0.9666). Stance
is the less saturated task, so the inductive bias from the auxiliary FPB
objective has more room to move the decision boundary.

\paragraph{(4) Careful general-purpose fine-tuning matches domain
pre-training.} Our BERT-base LLRD extension is effectively \emph{tied}
with Multi-task FinBERT: sentiment 0.9670 vs 0.9666 macro-F1 (BERT slightly
ahead), stance 0.6383 vs 0.6384 (multi-task slightly ahead by
$10^{-4}$). Weighted-F1 confirms the tie (multi-task: 0.6561 stance /
0.9780 sentiment; BERT LLRD: 0.6586 / 0.9759). This is a striking
specific-run finding: once we apply LLRD, gradual unfreezing, label
smoothing and class-weighted CE, a generic 2018 encoder reaches the same
operating point as a domain-pretrained encoder with an auxiliary head.
\emph{We do not claim LLRD outperforms FinBERT}--it matches it.

\paragraph{(5) The best non-neural system is still useful.}
TF-IDF + LinearSVC is 0.8534 sentiment / 0.6061 stance macro-F1, with
sub-millisecond inference. For latency-sensitive or air-gapped production
settings it is a defensible floor.

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:errors}

\paragraph{Per-class breakdown.} The Multi-task FinBERT model achieves
near-perfect F1 on the sentiment majority class (neutral 0.9928; negative
0.9500; positive 0.9569) but is markedly weaker on the two stance minority
classes (dovish 0.6174; hawkish 0.5869) than on the neutral stance class
(0.7109). BERT-base LLRD shows a similar pattern (dovish 0.5831; hawkish
0.6084; neutral 0.7235). Most stance errors are \emph{leaning~$\leftrightarrow$
neutral} confusions, not sign-flipped hawkish~$\leftrightarrow$~dovish
errors.

\paragraph{Sample FOMC stance errors.} The logged errors show the expected
pattern: hedged sentences drift into \emph{neutral} from the minority
classes, or vice-versa.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em,topsep=0.2em]
    \item ``\emph{To this end, the new statement conveys the Committee\ldots}''
          -- gold hawkish, predicted dovish.
    \item ``\emph{Looking ahead, reports from retailer contacts\ldots}''
          -- gold neutral, predicted dovish.
    \item ``\emph{Longer-term inflation expectations have moved\ldots}''
          -- gold hawkish, predicted neutral.
    \item ``\emph{In the Committee's discussion of current\ldots}''
          -- gold dovish, predicted neutral.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Sample PhraseBank sentiment errors.} Almost all remaining FPB
errors are on comparative profit/loss sentences whose polarity flips on a
``compared with'' clause:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.1em,itemsep=0.1em,topsep=0.2em]
    \item ``\emph{Operating loss was EUR 179mn, compared to a loss\ldots}''
          -- gold positive, predicted negative.
    \item ``\emph{In January--June 2010, diluted loss per share\ldots}''
          -- gold negative, predicted positive.
    \item ``\emph{Unit costs for flight operations fell by 6.4 percent\ldots}''
          -- gold positive, predicted negative.
\end{itemize}
The model latches onto local tokens (``loss'', ``fell'') and misses the
comparative framing.

\section{Deployment}
\label{sec:deploy}
We ship two interfaces. \texttt{predict.py} is a CLI that loads either the
fine-tuned single-task checkpoints or the Multi-task FinBERT and returns
JSON predictions on stdin. \texttt{demo.py} wraps the same model in a
Gradio web UI with two tabs (stance and sentiment), each showing the
top-1 label, per-class probabilities and the input sentence. A public
model card and checkpoint are released on the Hugging Face Hub for
reviewer reproducibility.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does multi-task help stance more?} Stance is the
less-saturated task. Sharing a FinBERT encoder with the much larger
sentiment signal regularises the stance head and supplies noisy but
abundant sentiment gradients that drag the shared representations
towards financially-meaningful directions. Once sentiment is close to
ceiling, there is no room for the auxiliary task to hurt it.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i) A 67\%-class stance accuracy ceiling
suggests sentence-level classification of FOMC prose is intrinsically
limited--dovish/hawkish often resolves only at the document or paragraph
level. (ii) We train 110M-parameter encoders; decoder-LLM approaches
with longer context windows may improve stance. (iii) We did not perform
data augmentation (paraphrasing, back-translation) which is a natural
next step given the small FOMC corpus.

\paragraph{Broader impact.} A financial stance/sentiment classifier
should be treated as \emph{decision support} for analysts, not as the
input to an autonomous trading strategy; misclassified FOMC sentences
can and do occur on material macroeconomic news.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We built twelve systems for financial sentiment and stance classification.
Sentiment is effectively solved at 97\%+ by any domain-pretrained or
carefully fine-tuned transformer, while stance tops out at 0.6384 macro-F1
with our Multi-task FinBERT--tied by a BERT-base model with
LLRD, gradual unfreezing and label smoothing. Multi-task learning gives
the largest relative lift on the harder task, and domain pre-training
remains most valuable in low-data regimes.
